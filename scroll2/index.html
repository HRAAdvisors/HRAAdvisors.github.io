The Ability to Work From Home is a Resiliency Strategy

By Kate Wittels

Working from home enables resilience for households, businesses and cities to withstand shocks, be them health, climate or safety related. As stay at home orders set in across the country, some Americans, usually those with more education and higher incomes, were able to easily transition to working from home. Whereas others, who were more likely black or brown, were unable to conduct their work virtually. For these Americans they either lost their source of income or were forced to expose themselves to COVID-19 in order to receive a paycheck. The stay at home orders have shed a spotlight on vast inequities that exist among different types of jobs – those that can be conducted with a computer and high-speed internet and those that cannot. As cities recover from COVID-19, investing in infrastructure, technology and training could enable more people to work from home, enabling a more just and resilient recovery.

As we recover from COVID-19 and prepare for future shocks, policy makers need to consider how to increase the ability for more Americans to work from home.
